SIMS Health Index


The SIMS Health Index is a powerful asset health monitoring tool that delivers clear, actionable insights into the condition of structural assets across your operation. By leveraging your existing operational data, it enables instant tracking, visualization, and analysis of asset condition, risk exposure, and maintenance costs all in one intuitive dashboard.


Developed in alignment with Anglo American’s SIMM Guidelines for Plant Structures and Conveyances, the SIMS Health Index brings a strategic management lens to structural integrity. It empowers decision-makers to balance risk, cost, and performance with confidence.


You already have the data. You already have the team. SIMS Health Index provides clarity, clarity that drives decisions, not just reports. It is more than dashboard, it’s a strategic decision support tool that enhances planning, improves outcomes, and strengthens performance. It doesn’t replace your current processes, it amplifies them. It’s familiar, easy to use, and designed to work with the systems and data you already rely on.

Condition


The fundamental purpose of plant structure and conveyance condition inspections is to confirm that the plant remains safe for productive use. Condition categories follow Anglo American’s SIMM Guidelines, while the extent rating indicates how common a particular defect is on the inspected item or area.


SIMS Condition Categories
Category Description Original Strength [%]
0 The plant structures are in excellent condition, with no deterioration evident. Safe use of the plant structures is assured. 100
1 The plant structures have slight evidence of surface deterioration, but to an extent that there is no reduction in strength. Safe use of the plant structures is assured. 100
2 The plant structures have some deterioration, to an extent that there is slight reduction in strength. Safe use of the plant structures is assured. 95 to 100
3 The plant structures show deterioration, to an extent that there is some reduction in strength. There is some compromise to safe use of the plant structures. Repair must receive attention in maintenance scheduling 75 to 95
4 The plant structures show severe deterioration, to an extent that there is a major reduction in strength. Safe use of the plant is severely compromised. Urgent attention must be given to repair. 50 to 75
5 The plant structures show severe deterioration, to an extent that they have little useful residual strength. Safe use of the plant is impossible. Urgent attention must be given to repair. <50

Condition Extent Ratings
Extent Description Coverage [%]
1 Defect is local. Isolated occurrence. <10
2 Defect is more than local. Occurs over parts of the item or area. 10 to 25
3 Defect is frequent over parts of the item or area. 25 to 50
4 Defect is general. Extensive occurrence over an area or item. >50

Risk


Remedial action priority levels and defect criticality levels are key elements in managing infrastructure asset risk. They support the assessment of defect severity and the urgency of response, based on potential impacts on safety, operations, and compliance. Priority levels are informed by an extension of Anglo American’s SIMM Guidelines and criticality levels are composed of factors such as safety implications, repair costs, and potential production losses associated with asset failure, where a level A has a higher threshold (more critcal) and a level D a lower threshold (less critical).


Levels of Priority
Priority Category Description
X High / Urgent Repair or replacement of the structural members must be completed before use of the structure.
Where a structure or portion of a structure cannot be used for its intended purpose in its current condition, but may not be required to be used for some time, it must be recorded as Priority X. This would typically apply to maintenance structures such as crawl beams.
1 High / Urgent Repair or replacement of the structural members, or other recommended work, requires urgent, immediate attention.
All members in condition category 5, any primary structural members in condition category 4, and any other members where the deterioration leads to a risk to personnel safety must be recorded as Priority 1.
2 Medium / Routine Repair or replacement of the structural members, or other recommended work, should be scheduled as soon as possible, but not later than one year from date of this report.
All secondary or tertiary members in condition category 4, and any other structural members requiring repair or replacement in the short term must be recorded as Priority 2.
3 Low / Deferred Repair of the structural members, or other recommended work, will be required within the next three years.
M Low / Deferred Monitoring - The condition of the structural member does not pose any foreseeable risk to the structure’s integrity. However, a monitoring programme must be put in place to ensure the condition does not further deteriorate over time.
RF Low / Deferred Run to Failure - Failure of a structure will not result in production loss or pose a safety risk and where the cost of ongoing maintenance exceeds the cost of replacement (including any associated production downtime), the structure may be allowed to run to failure. This approach is typically applied to dead load-bearing structures such as pump bases and pipe racks.
RR Medium / Routine Run to Replacement – When the cost of maintenance or rehabilitation, including associated production losses, exceeds the cost of replacement and its related downtime, the structure should be scheduled for replacement. This approach requires a strict monitoring regime and clearly defined replacement indices to trigger timely planning for replacement and shutdowns. It is typically applied to high-priority production conveyors and equipment, where modular components can be replaced during shorter shutdown periods with minimal impact on production.

Cost


SIMM audits generate detailed remedial action lists that serve as critical inputs into a mine’s structural maintenance and rehabilitation programme. These programmes typically include initiatives such as steel rehabilitation, corrosion protection, and concrete restoration, each comprising multiple projects with varying scopes and priorities.


The cost requirements for these projects are often significant and frequently exceed the available maintenance budget. As a result, prioritisation becomes essential. The SIMS Health Index plays a key role in this process by tracking actual investments made in structural maintenance and quantifying their impact on asset condition and risk exposure.


The ultimate objective is to invest strategically, allocating enough resources to reduce and stabilise structural risk below defined thresholds or KPIs, while observing the convergence of overall structural condition toward a manageable and sustainable state. This approach ensures that limited budgets are directed where they deliver the greatest operational and safety value.
